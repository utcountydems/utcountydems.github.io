faq_categories:
  - title: "Eligibility & Registration"
    icon: "fa-solid fa-circle-check"
    questions:
      - q: "Who can participate in the Democratic Caucus?"
        a: "Any registered Utah voter may participate in the Democratic Neighborhood Caucus Night — <strong>regardless of party affiliation</strong>. Unlike the Republican caucus, you do not need to be registered as a Democrat. If you are registered to vote in Utah, you are welcome."
      - q: "Do I need to be a registered Democrat?"
        a: "No. The Utah Democratic Party's caucus is open to all registered Utah voters. You do not need to change your voter registration or party affiliation to attend, vote, or run for a precinct position."
      - q: "How do I check or update my voter registration?"
        a: "Visit <a href=\"https://vote.utah.gov\" target=\"_blank\">vote.utah.gov</a> to check your registration status, update your address, or register to vote. You must be registered to participate in the caucus."
      - q: "What ID do I need to bring?"
        a: "None, just be prepared to check-in by finding your voter registration. Check-in opens at 6:30 PM."

  - title: "Attending the Caucus"
    icon: "fa-solid fa-people-group"
    questions:
      - q: "When and where is the caucus?"
        a: "Neighborhood Caucus Night is <strong>Tuesday, March 17, 2026</strong>. Check-in begins at <strong>6:30 PM</strong> and the official meeting starts at <strong>7:00 PM</strong>. Location varies by precinct — use the <a href=\"/precinct-locator.html\">Precinct Locator</a> to find your specific caucus site."
      - q: "How do I find my precinct?"
        a: "Use the <a href=\"/precinct-locator.html\">Precinct Locator</a> on this site to find your precinct by entering your address or using your device's location. You can also visit <a href=\"https://vote.utah.gov\" target=\"_blank\">vote.utah.gov</a> and use their voter lookup tool."
      - q: "What happens at the caucus?"
        a: "Neighbors from your precinct gather to elect local Democratic Party leaders: Precinct Chair, Vice-Chair, and Delegates. Candidates give brief speeches, answer questions, and then all attending voters cast ballots. The whole meeting typically lasts about an hour."
      - q: "What should I bring?"
        a: "Bring yourself and a friend along with something to write with, and optionally a notebook to evaluate candidates. Handout cards from candidates are common — don't be surprised if people share them with you."

  - title: "Running for a Position"
    icon: "fa-solid fa-person-chalkboard"
    questions:
      - q: "What positions are elected at the caucus?"
        a: "Three key positions are elected at the precinct caucus: <strong>Precinct Chair</strong> (leads the party in your neighborhood), <strong>Precinct Vice-Chair</strong> (assists and fills in for the Chair), and <strong>Delegates</strong> (attend county and state conventions to nominate candidates). All positions are two-year terms."
      - q: "Can I run for a position if I'm not a Democrat?"
        a: "Yes, any registered Utah voter may run for a precinct position, including Chair, Vice-Chair, and Delegate. You do not need to be registered as a Democrat. You may be asked to state your intention to support Democratic Party principles. You may not run for a precinct officer position if you hold party office in a different party."
      - q: "How do I announce my candidacy?"
        a: "All you have to do is simply announce at the caucus meeting itself when nominations are opened. The earlier you announce, the more time you have to meet neighbors."
      - q: "What does a Delegate do?"
        a: "Delegates represent your precinct at county and state nominating conventions, where they review and vote on candidates for public office. Under Democratic rules, a candidate must receive <strong>55% of delegate votes</strong> to win the nomination outright at convention. If no candidate reaches 55%, the top vote-getters proceed to a primary election."

  - title: "After the Caucus"
    icon: "fa-solid fa-forward"
    questions:
      - q: "What happens after caucus night?"
        a: "Newly elected Delegates attend county and state nominating conventions in April, where they vote to nominate Democratic candidates for the 2026 general election. Precinct Chairs and Vice-Chairs begin serving immediately and attend County Central Committee meetings throughout the year."
      - q: "What are the convention nomination rules?"
        a: "The Utah Democratic Party uses a <strong>55% threshold</strong> at convention for a candidate to win the nomination without a primary. If no candidate receives 55%, the top two vote-getters advance to a Democratic primary election. This differs from the Republican party, which uses a 50%+1 majority threshold."
      - q: "What are Issue & Identity Caucuses?"
        a: "The Democratic Party has a network of caucuses organized around shared identities and issues — including Stonewall Democrats (LGBTQIA+), LDS Democrats, BIPOC Caucus, Healthcare Caucus, Progressive Caucus, Rural Utah Caucus, Seniors Caucus, and Young Democrats. These groups meet year-round and have delegates at state conventions. Visit the <a href=\"/caucuses.html\">Issue Caucuses page</a> to learn more."
